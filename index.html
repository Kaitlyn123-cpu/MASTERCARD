Brand Guidelines

The system is designed to be consistent (recognizable) and flexible (endless variations).

Brand story

Mastercard is a global payments network built on trust and connection. Our system visualizes the brand through protective forms and shared overlap: two shields create a stable core identity that reads as secure and dependable, while motion and generative variation communicate constant activity, scale, and adaptability across platforms.

Audience

  • Consumers: digital-first users who expect speed and clarity.
  • Merchants: businesses that need reliability and consistency.
  • Partners: fintech and developers building on payment infrastructure.
  • Context: screens, signage, social assets, and brand touchpoints.

Colour rule: red and orange must always overlap at least partially. Transparency can be used to emphasize the shared overlap zone (“connection”).

Typography

  • Style: clean geometric sans-serif to match clarity and trust.
  • Hierarchy: uppercase headlines + short body copy.
  • Use: type anchors the layout; motion carries expression.

Recommended (free) web font options: Inter or Montserrat. (If you don’t want to load fonts, system fonts are acceptable for this assignment.)

Design system rules

  • Core form: always two shields (red + gold).
  • Overlap: required—this is the “identity anchor.”
  • Protection: shield silhouette reinforces trust and security.
  • Flow: particles represent transactions/network activity.
  • Motion: sine/noise-based movement for smooth reliability.
  • Variation: a numeric seed controls randomness for reproducibility.

Outputs mapping

  • Logo (200×200): minimal, centered, instantly recognizable.
  • Poster (1400×2100): higher density; “frozen moment” of motion.
  • Animated screen (1080×1920): looping flow for signage/splash screens.

Process

How we connected brand meaning to a generative system and produced final deliverables.

Goals

  • Primary: Make the identity readable instantly (overlapping shields = security + connection).
  • Secondary: Communicate “flow” and “network movement” through motion and particles.
  • Success criteria: Consistent brand recognition across multiple seeds + clean compositions at all sizes.

Audience & context

  • Audience: digital-first consumers, merchants, and fintech partners.
  • Use cases: digital signage, app/splash environments, posters, social assets.
  • Need: fast scanning, high contrast, and a flexible identity that scales.

Key design decisions

  • Constraint first: we locked the overlapping shield rule before adding variation.
  • Composition: centered logo, structured poster layout, full-bleed screen feel.
  • Hierarchy: bold headline + minimal copy so visuals stay dominant.
  • Consistency: neutral backgrounds keep brand colours crisp and recognizable.

Iteration & evaluation

  • What we tested: shield spacing, overlap strength, and particle density.
  • What improved: clearer overlap zone and more controlled particle field for readability.
  • Why it’s better: maintains identity at a glance while still showing motion/flow at larger scales.

Technical implementation (p5.js)

  • Objects: shields and particles coded as reusable components.
  • Arrays + loops: manage and render particle systems efficiently.
  • Motion: sine/noise to keep movement smooth (trustworthy, not chaotic).
  • Seeds: enable reproducible outputs for consistent brand variants.

Proof it’s a design system

  • Same rules generate every output.
  • Different seeds create new variations without breaking recognition.
  • Different sizes show the system scales from logo → poster → screen.

Reflection

A short, grading-friendly reflection on outcomes and learning.

What worked: Building strict constraints first (two shields + overlap) gave us strong brand recognition immediately. After that, particles and motion could vary freely without losing the identity.

What changed through iteration: We reduced visual noise by controlling particle density and keeping backgrounds neutral, which made the overlap zone more readable—especially at poster scale.

Next steps: We would test the system across more contexts (mobile UI, social crops, signage distance) and define seed “sets” for specific campaigns or regions.
